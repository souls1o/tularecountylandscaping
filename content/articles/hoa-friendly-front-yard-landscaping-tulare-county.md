HOAs rarely oppose thoughtful landscaping—they oppose **surprises** that shift drainage, hardscape percentages, or sight triangles without documentation. Front-yard upgrades across Tulare County go smoother when drawings, plant lists, finish samples, and irrigation notes arrive **before** demolition starts, especially in communities where architectural committees review plant heights and fence-adjacent setbacks.

Water-wise palettes can remain covenant-friendly when evergreens provide structure, edges stay crisp, and mulch reads maintained through dormant seasons—priorities that show up frequently around [Exeter](/locations/exeter-ca) and [Dinuba](/locations/dinuba-ca). The winning submissions usually speak the HOA’s language: measurable heights, labeled materials, and photo context of existing grades.

> **Key takeaway:** Package compliance early—drainage narratives, plant heights, and fixture shielding—so reviewers see stewardship, not risk.

---

## Irrigation, drainage narratives, and gravel transitions

Many associations ask whether proposed work alters drainage toward neighbors or common walkways. If your plan adjusts grade or replaces turf with [gravel landscaping](/services/gravel-landscaping), simple spot elevations and photo sequences help reviewers understand intent.

Pair narrative clarity with practical irrigation disclosure: where spray arcs change, whether downspouts or apron slopes redirect runoff, and how beds will be watered after turf removal.
