## Pressure changes, knocking pipes, and hose-bib clues

When outdoor pressure drops after the system pressurizes—or you hear pipes knocking when zones cycle—there may be a leak, stuck valve, or partially crushed lateral line. These symptoms often worsen under peak demand, when every zone is fighting for the same supply.

Document what you hear and when it happens: which zone is running, whether the master valve or pump relay is involved, and whether pressure returns when irrigation is off. That timeline helps technicians distinguish supply problems from valve failures before they start digging.

---

## Wet pockets, fungus rings, and chronic overspray

Chronic wet pockets invite algae on concrete, fungus in turf, and root rot in ornamentals. These symptoms frequently trace back to misaligned heads, stuck diaphragms, outdated spray arcs, or heads that no longer pop to grade after years of mower bumps.

Uneven coverage maps—dry corners near lot lines and strips along fences—often mean **matched precipitation rate** problems: rotary and spray zones mixed incorrectly, wrong nozzles for the pressure available, or pressure that runs too high for small arcs. When beds need reliable moisture without spraying stucco or vinyl fence boards, many homeowners eventually split turf from shrubs and route planting areas to drip; your matched contractor can map that transition alongside [irrigation repair](/services/irrigation-repair).

---

## Controllers, wiring, and “ghost” zones

If programs advance unpredictably or a zone runs when nothing shows “on,” wiring faults or master valve problems may be involved. Photograph the controller face, note any error codes, and list zones that misbehave only during certain programs—irrigation diagnostics move faster when homeowners capture observations instead of relying on memory during a mid-day visit.
